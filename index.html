Strategic Recommendations

1. Pricing Updates for High-Demand Opponents

The secondary market reveals significant willingness to pay above face value for certain opponents. The Strykers should consider primary pricing adjustments for these matchups:

Washington ($404 ATP, $295 without promotions): Even controlling for promotions, WSH games command nearly 3x the overall ATP. This is the clearest signal that primary prices are undervalued for this matchup. The Strykers should implement premium pricing tiers for WSH games â€” the secondary market data supports a significant increase.

Baltimore ($127 ATP, identical with and without promotions): BAL demand is genuine and promotion-independent. Promo ATP ($126.83) and non-promo ATP ($126.78) are virtually identical, confirming this is a rivalry-driven premium. Primary prices should reflect this 26% above-average demand.

New York ($118 ATP, 0% promotions): NY games have zero promotions yet still command 17% above average. This is pure organic demand likely driven by the large visiting fanbase. Primary pricing should be adjusted upward, and no promotional investment is needed for these games.

Philadelphia ($116 ATP, 100% promotions): PHL games have always coincided with promotions, so we cannot fully isolate the baseline demand. However, given the $116 ATP even with promo-inflated comparisons, PHL is likely a genuine mid-to-high demand opponent. The Strykers should test a non-promo PHL game to establish true baseline demand, but a moderate primary price increase is likely warranted.

Memphis ($120 ATP, 0% promotions): Similar to NY, MEM generates above-average pricing organically. A moderate primary price increase is supported by the data.

2. Promotions for Low-Demand Opponents

The bottom tier of opponents (all under $30 ATP) currently receive little to no promotional support. Given that promotions add an average of $82.59 per ticket in Model 3, strategically deploying giveaways to these matchups could significantly boost revenue:

St. Louis ($26 ATP, 0% promo): The lowest-demand opponent receives zero promotions. Adding a hat giveaway (associated with $198 mean prices) could dramatically lift secondary market activity and fan turnout.

Indianapolis ($29 ATP, 0% promo), San Diego ($29, 0%), Milwaukee ($29, 0%): These opponents cluster around $29 ATP with no promotional support. Even lower-cost promotions like posters ($125 mean) could meaningfully improve demand.

Houston ($43 ATP, 100% promo), Seattle ($32, 100%), Buffalo ($31, 100%): These opponents already receive promotions but still show very low ATPs. This indicates that the promotion type or the underlying matchup interest is insufficient. The Strykers should consider pairing these games with enhanced fan experience elements (theme nights, fireworks, concerts) rather than solely relying on giveaway items.

3. Other Factors to Increase Revenue and Fan Demand

Day-of-week scheduling: Sunday games command the highest ATP ($164) â€” 4.8x higher than Friday games ($34). Where scheduling flexibility exists, prioritizing high-demand opponents on Sundays maximizes revenue. Low-demand opponents should be paired with weekday promotions.

Dynamic pricing by purchase window: The $45.13 gap between Planner and Last-Minute ATP suggests implementing early-bird incentives (small discounts or seat upgrades for advance purchasers) to shift the buying curve earlier. The revenue model shows this could generate $169,229 annually.

Promotion quality matters more than frequency: Hat giveaways average $198 vs T-Shirt nights at $33. The type of promotion significantly affects secondary market value. Investing in higher-quality giveaway items, especially for mid-tier opponents, likely has better ROI than simply increasing the number of promo nights.

Halftime experience upgrades: The $10 section upgrade program could generate $197,470 per season as an entirely new revenue stream. This also improves the fan experience by allowing access to premium seating at low cost, encouraging future full-price purchases in those sections.

Lower Sideline premium: The 2.37x price gap between Lower Sideline ($232) and Lower Goal Line ($98) at the same bowl level indicates the market highly values the sideline viewing angle. Primary pricing should better reflect this differentiation.
